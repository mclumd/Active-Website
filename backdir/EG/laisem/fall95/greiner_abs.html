TITLE: ``Learning Good Search Strategies''

ABSTRACT:

Many tasks, including derivation, diagnosis and planning, require searching through a network of probabilistic experiments, seeking a satisfying set of successful outcomes. A "search strategy" specifies the order in which to perform these experiments; we clearly prefer a strategy whose "expected cost" is minimal. This expected cost depends on the success probabilities of the experiments, which unfortunately are usually not known a priori. This work first describes statistical methods for estimating these probabilities, and then provides two methods that use these estimates to obtain good strategies: One returns a strategy whose expected cost is, with high probability, approximately optimal. As this process is not always tractable, we next present a more general method that efficiently hill-climbs to a strategy that is, with high probability, close to a local optimum. These techniques directly address the "utility problem" of explanation-based learning systems. We also demonstrate their generality by showing how they can be used to address other (non-search) tasks, including

  • theory revision,
  • the "multiple-extension" problem of non-monotonic reasoning,
  • landmark selection for autonomous navigation, and
  • the "tractability / completeness" problem from knowledge representation.
These results are an application of the ideas of statistics and formal learning theory to concrete knowledge representation tasks.

Short Biography for Russell Greiner:

After receiving his PhD in Computer Science from Stanford, Russell Greiner worked at the University of Toronto (as a visiting professor) before moving to Siemens Corporate Research. He has over 60 publications, in topics including

  • machine learning
    • analogical inference
    • explanation-based learning
    • formal PAC-learning
    • theory revision
    • dealing with missing information
  • vision and robot navigation
  • knowledge representation
    • efficient reasoning (optimal derivational strategies, caching/memoizing, finding effective abstractions)
    • diagnosis
    • plan verification
  • databases
  • operations research
  • signal analysis
  • systems and control theory.

The main foci of his current work are applying machine learning techniques to address specific knowledge representation tasks, effective theory revision, understanding "relevance", and robot navigation.
