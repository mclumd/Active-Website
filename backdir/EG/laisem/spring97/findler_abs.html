TITLE: ``Distributed Artificial Intelligence -- Theory and Applications''

ABSTRACT:

Distributed Artificial Intelligence (DAI) is a rapidly growing and relatively new area within Artificial Intelligence. It is usually divided into two categories of research activity: Distributed Planning and Problem Solving Systems and Multi-Agent Systems. The former references systems in which the individual nodes (agents, computers) fully cooperate whereas the latter category contains systems in which the agents are heterogeneous (different capabilities, beliefs, goals, values, attitudes, commitments, etc.) and may compete with each.

The talk will provide a theoretical introduction to DAI and describe briefly six projects completed:

. Future Air Traffic Control Systems,
. Distributed Control of Nationwide Manufacturing Operations,
. Distributed Control of Allocating Moving Resources to Moving Tasks,
. Distributed Control of Street and Highway Ramp Traffic Signals,
. Coordination and Cooperation in Air War Environments,
. Alliances and Norms in Societies of Heterogeneous Interacting Agents.

RESUME:

Nicholas V. Findler is Research Professor of Computer Science, Director of the Artificial Intelligence Laboratory and Adjunct Professor of Mathematics at Arizona State University. He has worked in various areas of Artificial Intelligence since 1957 and has authored or co-authored over 180 refereed articles and wrote/edited/contributed to 38 books.

His current interests include Artificial Intelligence, Simulation of Cognitive Behavior, Heuristic Programming, Decision Making under Uncert- ainty and Risk, Theory of Strategies, Computational Linguistics, Information Retrieval, and Expert Systems. He was the Director of the First NATO Advanced Study Institute on Artificial Intelligence and Heuristic Programming and has lectured at eight NATO Advanced Study Institutes. During his sabbaticals, he was a Senior Fulbright Scholar and Visiting Professor at the Technical University of Vienna, Austria in 1972-73; a Senior Fulbright Scholar and Visiting Professor at the University of Amsterdam and the Free University of Amsterdam, The Nether- lands, Visiting Professor at the European Institute of Advanced Studies in Management, Brussels and Invited Scholar at the College Interuniver- sitaire d'Etudes Doctorales dans les Sciences du Management, University of Liege, Belgium, 1979-80; Visiting Professor of Computer Science at the University of Zurich, Switzerland, Senior Fulbright Scholar and Visiting Professor at the Hungarian Academy of Sciences and the Eotvos Lorand University, Budapest, Hungary, and NATO Research Visitor at the University of Copenhagen, Denmark, 1988-89; Visiting Professor at LAFORIA and Visiting Director of Research at the CNRS, University Pierre and Marie Curie, Paris, France, 1995.

He has been an ACM Visiting Scientist and Visiting Lecturer, USIA Lecturer, Member of the IEEE Outstanding Lecturers program. His awards include the ACM Recognition of Service Award, Fellow of the British Computer Society, Senior Member of the IEEE, Outstanding Educator of America, and various recognitions from universities in North and South America, Europe, Asia and Oceania.
