TITLE: ``Human Belief Revision''

ABSTRACT:

Belief revision is seen as the required change to an initial set of beliefs, modeled as a set of propositional sentences, when new information presents an inconsistency with the initial set. Certain prescriptive notions of belief revision, such as epistemic entrenchment and minimal change (which are common in the AI literature) depend on the representational assumptions adopted for modeling the belief state, in particular whether a syntactic or model-theoretic perspective is taken. These perspectives were examined by manipulating the syntactic form of sentences comprising the initial belief state that subjects had. When new and inconsistent information was encountered, subjects were to select among several alternative sets of sentences as their preferred new sets of beliefs....the alternatives differing according to which of the initial beliefs were abandoned in order to define a new consistent belief set.

Four definitions of a minimal change in belief state (from the AI literature) are surveyed and it is shown that subjects do not follow any of them. Further, subjects always preferred to eliminate logical inconsistency by abandoning conditional statements rather than non-conditional ones, even though either option would lead to a consistent new belief state. Revision choices were also affected by whether the initial belief state included a modus ponens or modus tollens inference, and whether the problems involved abstract symbols or natural language cover stories.

Various issues are discussed involving the relevance of empirical evidence of studies like these for notions like minimal change and epistemic entrenchment in both prescriptive and descriptive principles of belief revision.

Jeff Pelletier received a BS in Math and an MA in Philosophy at the University of Nebraska. He received his PhD at UCLA, where he wrote a dissertation on "Mass Terms", a subject which continues to hold his interest. He also holds master's degrees in Linguistics and Computer Science from the University of Alberta. He currently is Professor of Philosophy and of Computing Science at the University of Alberta.

Much of his writing in philosophical logic/formal semantics is on such topics as vagueness and the correct representation of "interesting" natural language constructions like Generics and Mass Terms. More recently he has become interested in such Cognitive Science topics such as how people deal with "belief revisions" and "default reasoning." He is currently working on a book called 'Non Standard Methods for Automated Reasoning in Non Standard Logics' and is editor of `The Generic Book'. He has been the Executive Editor for *Canadian Journal of Philosophy* and `Linguistics and Philosophy', and is on the editorial boards of several other journals. He has also written a book and edited anthologies in Ancient Greek Philosophy.

Everyone is welcome to attend.
